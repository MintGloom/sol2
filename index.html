Section II — Long Answer Questions [5 × 10 = 50 Marks]
  1. Describe the three mechanisms of nutrient transport from soil to plant roots. Explain the factors that influence the relative contribution of each mechanism.
    Unit I | 10 marks | ~600 words
    Model Answer

    Nutrients move from the bulk soil to the root surface via three distinct mechanisms:

    1. Root Interception (~1–2% of total uptake)

    As roots elongate through the soil, they physically contact nutrient ions already present in soil solution or on exchange sites. The root essentially "grows into" the nutrient. This mechanism is most significant for calcium and magnesium in fine-textured soils and is limited to the small volume of soil displaced by the expanding root.

    2. Mass Flow (Convective Transport, ~80–95% of N, ~50–60% of Ca/Mg)

    Water movement toward the root surface (driven by transpiration) carries dissolved nutrients in the soil solution. The flux of nutrients = water flux × nutrient concentration in soil solution. High transpiration, low soil water potential, and high nutrient concentration in solution all enhance mass flow. Nitrate and calcium are primarily supplied by this mechanism. If supply via mass flow exceeds demand, accumulation may occur at the root surface.

    3. Diffusion (~80% of P and K supply)

    When root uptake depletes nutrient concentration at the root surface, a concentration gradient forms from bulk soil to root. Nutrients diffuse along this gradient following Fick's first law: J = –D · (dC/dx), where J = flux, D = diffusion coefficient, and dC/dx = concentration gradient. Diffusion is extremely slow (especially for P: D ≈ 10⁻¹²–10⁻¹⁵ m²/s) and operates over very short distances (~1–2 mm). Phosphorus and potassium depend heavily on diffusion.

    Factors Affecting Contribution of Each Mechanism

    • Soil moisture: Low moisture reduces mass flow and diffusion (tortuosity increases).
    • Soil texture: Clay soils have lower diffusion coefficients due to restricted water-filled pores.
    • Temperature: Higher temperatures increase diffusion coefficients and transpiration rate.
    • Root morphology: Dense, branching roots and root hairs increase interception and absorption surface.
    • Transpiration rate: Directly drives mass flow; reduced under stomatal closure.
    • Nutrient buffering capacity: High buffering (e.g., P fixation) reduces diffusion gradient replenishment.
    • Mycorrhizal associations: Fungal hyphae extend the effective root surface, dramatically improving phosphorus uptake via diffusion.

    Conclusion: Most field crops obtain N primarily through mass flow, P and K through diffusion, and only a minor fraction through interception. Understanding these mechanisms is critical for optimizing placement, timing, and form of fertilizer application.

  2. Discuss the chemistry of soil phosphorus, its various fractions, and the factors affecting phosphorus availability to crops.
    Unit III | 10 marks | ~600 words
    Model Answer

    Forms of Soil Phosphorus

    Total soil P ranges from 200–3000 mg/kg but only 0.01–1.0 mg/L exists in solution at any time.

    • Soil solution P: Primarily H₂PO₄⁻ (acidic soils) and HPO₄²⁻ (neutral-alkaline soils). Only this fraction is directly absorbed.
    • Labile P: Loosely sorbed P on surfaces of Fe/Al oxides and clay minerals; in equilibrium with solution P.
    • Non-labile P: Strongly chemisorbed or occluded within iron oxide coatings; released very slowly.
    • Mineral P: Apatites [Ca₁₀(PO₄)₆(OH,F,Cl)₂] in alkaline soils; variscite (AlPO₄·2H₂O) and strengite (FePO₄·2H₂O) in acid soils.
    • Organic P: Comprises 30–80% of total P; includes inositol phosphates, phospholipids, nucleic acids; mineralized by phosphatase enzymes.

    P Fixation Reactions

    In acid soils: P reacts with Fe³⁺ and Al³⁺ ions and their hydroxides to form insoluble precipitates (ligand exchange). In alkaline soils: P reacts with Ca²⁺ to form dicalcium phosphate (DCPD), then hydroxyapatite. Both reactions reduce plant-available P.

    Factors Affecting P Availability

    • Soil pH: Maximum availability at pH 6–7. Below 5.5, Al and Fe fixation dominate; above 7.5, Ca fixation increases.
    • Temperature: Cold soils reduce P diffusion and root metabolic activity.
    • Soil organic matter: Humic substances compete for adsorption sites, reducing P fixation; also mineralization releases organic P.
    • Ca, Fe, Al content: High concentrations increase fixation.
    • Mycorrhizal fungi: Arbuscular mycorrhiza (AM fungi) extend the phosphorus depletion zone 5–7 cm beyond the root, mobilizing P from depletion zones.
    • Redox potential: Under flooding, Fe³⁺ → Fe²⁺ releases P (P availability increases in waterlogged rice soils).

    Management Implications

    Band placement reduces contact between P fertilizer and soil (lowering fixation), liming acid soils to pH 6–6.5 reduces Al/Fe fixation, applying P with organic matter, and inoculation with mycorrhizal fungi are key strategies to improve P use efficiency.

  3. Classify chemical fertilizers and describe in detail the properties, nitrogen content, and agronomic use of major nitrogenous fertilizers used in India.
    Unit IV | 10 marks | ~600 words
    Model Answer

    Classification of Chemical Fertilizers

    Fertilizers are broadly classified as: (i) Straight fertilizers — supply one primary nutrient; (ii) Complex fertilizers — chemically combined two or more nutrients; (iii) Mixed fertilizers — physical mixture of straights; (iv) Multinutrient fertilizers including micronutrient-enriched; (v) Nano fertilizers.

    Major Nitrogenous Fertilizers

    • Urea [CO(NH₂)₂] — 46% N: Most widely used globally. Hydrolyzes rapidly to NH₄⁺ via urease enzyme. Neutral reaction, but can cause ammonia volatilization in high-pH soils. Should be incorporated into soil. Suitable for all crops and soils.
    • Ammonium Sulphate [(NH₄)₂SO₄] — 20.6% N: Contains 24% S. Physiologically acidic; especially suitable for alkaline soils and S-deficient soils. Lower N content; heavier transport. Reduces soil pH over time.
    • Ammonium Nitrate [NH₄NO₃] — 33–34% N: Contains both NH₄⁺ and NO₃⁻ forms; excellent for quick effect. Explosive/hygroscopic — restricted in India due to safety concerns. Mainly used in fertigation.
    • Calcium Ammonium Nitrate (CAN) — 25–26% N: Granular, non-explosive, contains Ca. Suitable for acidic soils.
    • Ammonium Chloride (NH₄Cl) — 26% N: Physiologically acidic; not suitable for Cl-sensitive crops (tobacco, potato). Used in paddy.
    • Slow-release N fertilizers: Neem-coated urea (NCU), sulphur-coated urea (SCU), NBPT-treated urea — reduce volatilization and nitrification losses; improve NUE by 10–15%.

    N Losses and Management

    N can be lost via volatilization (NH₃, especially from surface-applied urea), nitrification + denitrification (NO₃⁻ → N₂O/N₂ under anaerobic conditions), and leaching (NO₃⁻). Best practices: deep placement, split applications, urease/nitrification inhibitors, fertigation.

    Agronomic Considerations

    Time of application should coincide with peak crop demand; basal dose of ammoniacal N, top-dressing of nitrate N for cereals. Fertigation with ammonium nitrate or urea solutions maximizes efficiency in drip-irrigated crops.

  4. What are organic manures? Describe the preparation of compost by the Indore method and discuss its agronomic significance.
    Unit V | 10 marks | ~600 words
    Model Answer

    Organic Manures — Introduction

    Organic manures are substances of plant and/or animal origin that supply nutrients and improve soil physical, chemical, and biological properties. They include bulky manures (FYM, compost, green manure — low nutrient % but large volumes) and concentrated manures (oil cakes, blood meal, fish meal — high nutrient %).

    Compost Preparation — Indore Method

    Developed at the Institute of Plant Industry, Indore (now ICAR-IIWBR) by Sir Albert Howard. Steps:

    1. Site selection: Shade, near water source, on slightly elevated ground.
    2. Pit construction: Pits 3 m × 1.5 m × 1 m (L × W × D) or surface heaps.
    3. Layering: Layer of 15 cm plant refuse → 5 cm of cattle dung/urine earth → thin layer of soil. Repeat to fill pit. Wet with water at each layer. Final layer is soil.
    4. C:N ratio adjustment: Mix leguminous and non-leguminous material; N-rich materials (dung, urine) accelerate decomposition.
    5. Turning: Pit is turned (mixed) at 15 days, 30 days, and 60 days to ensure aeration and uniform decomposition. Water is added if dry (moisture ~50–60%).
    6. Maturation: Compost is ready in 3–4 months. Well-decomposed compost is dark brown, crumbly, earthy-smelling with C:N ratio ≤20:1.

    Nutrient Composition of Compost

    Good quality compost contains approximately: 1.5–2.0% N, 0.5–0.8% P₂O₅, 1.0–1.5% K₂O, with a wide range of micronutrients and secondary nutrients.

    Agronomic Significance

    • Soil physical properties: Improves soil structure (aggregate stability), reduces bulk density, increases water-holding capacity.
    • Soil chemical properties: Acts as a pH buffer; supplies slow-release nutrients; increases CEC.
    • Biological activity: Provides substrate for soil microorganisms; increases microbial biomass, enzyme activity, and diversity.
    • Sustainability: Recycles farm wastes; reduces dependence on chemical fertilizers; contributes to carbon sequestration.
    • Under INM: Combining compost with reduced chemical fertilizer doses maintains or improves crop yields while protecting soil health.
  5. Explain the concept of Nutrient Use Efficiency (NUE). Discuss the factors affecting NUE and the strategies to improve it under irrigated and rainfed conditions.
    Unit VI | 10 marks | ~600 words
    Model Answer

    Definition of NUE

    Nutrient Use Efficiency (NUE) is broadly defined as the ratio of crop output (yield or nutrient output in grain/biomass) to nutrient input (fertilizer applied + soil supply). Several indices are used:

    • Agronomic efficiency (AE): Yield increase per unit nutrient applied = (Yf − Y₀) / F
    • Recovery efficiency (RE): Proportion of applied nutrient taken up by the crop.
    • Physiological efficiency (PE): Grain yield per unit nutrient taken up.
    • Apparent recovery: (Nutrient uptake in treated − control) / nutrient applied × 100. Typical N recovery in India: 30–50%.

    Factors Affecting NUE

    • Soil factors: pH, texture, organic matter, moisture, fixation capacity (P in acidic soils), waterlogging (denitrification).
    • Crop factors: Root architecture, root hair development, mycorrhizal association, genetic capacity to absorb and translocate nutrients.
    • Fertilizer management: Source, dose, time, and method of application significantly affect NUE.
    • Climate: Rainfall timing, temperature, solar radiation (affect uptake and transport).

    Strategies to Improve NUE

    Irrigated conditions:

    • Fertigation (drip/sprinkler) — delivers nutrients directly to root zone with water, improving efficiency by 20–40%.
    • Split application of N synchronized with crop growth stages.
    • Leaf color chart (LCC) or SPAD-meter guided N application.
    • Use of slow-release and controlled-release fertilizers (coated urea, nano fertilizers).
    • Use of urease inhibitors (NBPT) and nitrification inhibitors (DCD, neem coating).

    Rainfed conditions:

    • Apply fertilizers in anticipation of rainfall (soil moisture–based recommendations).
    • Placement in moist soil zones (subsurface band placement) to avoid surface loss.
    • Integrated nutrient management — organic + inorganic to buffer nutrient release.
    • Crop varieties with higher nutrient uptake efficiency under stress.
    • Contingency crop planning aligned with rainfall patterns.

    Soil Testing and Plant Analysis for NUE Optimization

    Soil testing (before sowing) and rapid plant tissue tests (mid-season) allow dynamic adjustment of fertilizer doses to actual crop needs, minimizing waste and maximizing recovery. Soil fertility maps enable site-specific nutrient management (precision agriculture), further boosting NUE in large farms.

Section II — Long Answer Questions [5 × 10 = 50 Marks]
  1. Define essential plant nutrients and explain the criteria of essentiality. Classify all 17 essential elements with the forms in which they are absorbed by plants.
    Unit I & II | 10 marks
    Model Answer

    Essential Plant Nutrients

    An element is considered essential for plant growth if it satisfies all three criteria proposed by Arnon and Stout (1939):

    1. A deficiency of the element makes it impossible for the plant to complete its vegetative or reproductive stage.
    2. The deficiency is specific to the element concerned and can be prevented or corrected only by supplying that element.
    3. The element is directly involved in the nutrition of the plant (structural, enzymatic, or osmotic role), not merely correcting some unfavorable soil condition.

    Classification of 17 Essential Elements

    A. Obtained from Air and Water (3):
    Carbon (CO₂), Hydrogen (H₂O), Oxygen (O₂/H₂O)

    B. Primary Macronutrients (3) — required in large amounts:

    • Nitrogen — absorbed as NO₃⁻ and NH₄⁺
    • Phosphorus — absorbed as H₂PO₄⁻ and HPO₄²⁻
    • Potassium — absorbed as K⁺

    C. Secondary Macronutrients (3):

    • Calcium — absorbed as Ca²⁺
    • Magnesium — absorbed as Mg²⁺
    • Sulphur — absorbed as SO₄²⁻

    D. Micronutrients (8) — required in trace amounts:

    • Iron — Fe²⁺ (ferrous) or Fe³⁺-chelate
    • Manganese — Mn²⁺
    • Zinc — Zn²⁺
    • Copper — Cu²⁺ or Cu⁺
    • Boron — H₃BO₃ or B(OH)₄⁻
    • Molybdenum — MoO₄²⁻
    • Chlorine — Cl⁻
    • Nickel — Ni²⁺ (essential for urease; added later by Brown, 1987)

    Law of Minimum

    Liebig's Law of Minimum states that plant growth is controlled not by the total amount of nutrients available, but by the scarcest (limiting) nutrient. The "barrel analogy" illustrates that yield is limited by the shortest stave. This principle underlies balanced fertilization — correcting the most deficient nutrient gives maximum response.

  2. Describe the role, deficiency symptoms, and toxicity symptoms of nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium in plants. What are the critical soil levels for each?
    Unit II | 10 marks
    Model Answer

    Nitrogen (N)

    Role: Structural component of amino acids, proteins, nucleic acids (DNA, RNA), chlorophyll, alkaloids, and coenzymes. Essential for vegetative growth, protein synthesis, and enzyme activity.

    Deficiency: Yellowing (chlorosis) of older leaves first (mobile element); stunted growth; reduced tillering; light green to pale yellow plant; premature senescence. In maize: characteristic "V"-shaped yellowing from leaf tip.

    Toxicity: Excess N causes excessive vegetative growth (lodging); delayed maturity; lush, dark green plants susceptible to diseases; accumulation of NO₃⁻ in plant tissue; reduced fruit set.

    Critical soil level: Low (<280 kg/ha available N), Medium (280–560), High (>560 kg/ha) as per Subbiah and Asija alkaline KMnO₄ method.

    Phosphorus (P)

    Role: Component of ATP/ADP (energy currency), nucleic acids, phospholipids (cell membranes), phytin (seed storage), promotes root development, flowering, and fruiting.

    Deficiency: Stunted growth; purplish/reddish discoloration of older leaves (anthocyanin accumulation due to sugar buildup); delayed maturity; poor root development; thin stems. Maize: purple coloration of leaves/stems.

    Toxicity: Rare in soil; excess P induces Zn, Fe, and Cu deficiency (antagonism); luxury uptake reduces P use efficiency.

    Critical soil level: Low (<11 kg/ha available P₂O₅), Medium (11–22), High (>22 kg/ha) by Olsen/Bray method.

    Potassium (K)

    Role: Not a structural component; regulates osmotic potential, stomatal opening/closure, activates 60+ enzymes, promotes starch synthesis, protein synthesis, phloem loading of sugars, and disease resistance.

    Deficiency: Marginal/tip scorch (necrosis) of older leaves first; weak stems; reduced seed/fruit quality; higher disease susceptibility; lodging in cereals.

    Toxicity: Rare; excess K causes Mg and Ca deficiency through ion antagonism.

    Critical soil level: Low (<110 kg/ha available K₂O), Medium (110–280), High (>280 kg/ha) by ammonium acetate extraction.

  3. Write a detailed account of the chemistry of soil nitrogen — its forms, transformations, and losses. How can nitrogen management be improved to minimize environmental losses?
    Unit III | 10 marks
    Model Answer

    Forms of Soil Nitrogen

    Total soil N ranges from 0.02–0.5%. About 95–99% is organic N (humus, microbial biomass, plant residues) and only 1–5% is inorganic (mineral) N (NH₄⁺ and NO₃⁻).

    • Organic N: Amino acids, amino sugars, nucleic acids, humic compounds — not directly available; released through mineralization.
    • NH₄⁺ (ammonium): Fixed in clay lattices (non-exchangeable), exchangeable on CEC sites, or in soil solution. Relatively stable.
    • NO₃⁻ (nitrate): Highly mobile in soil solution; major form in well-aerated soils; subject to leaching and denitrification.

    N Transformations

    • Mineralization (ammonification): Organic N → NH₄⁺ via soil microorganisms. Promoted by warm, moist, aerated, neutral pH conditions.
    • Nitrification: NH₄⁺ → NO₂⁻ (Nitrosomonas) → NO₃⁻ (Nitrobacter). Aerobic, 20–30°C, pH 6–8.
    • Immobilization: Inorganic N incorporated into microbial biomass when C:N >30. Competes with plants.
    • Denitrification: NO₃⁻ → N₂O → N₂ by facultative anaerobes under waterlogged conditions. Major loss pathway in rice paddies.
    • N fixation: Symbiotic (Rhizobium, 50–300 kg N/ha/yr) and non-symbiotic (Azotobacter, Azospirillum).
    • Leaching: NO₃⁻ moves downward with water; major concern in coarse soils with excess rainfall/irrigation.
    • Volatilization: NH₃ loss from surface-applied urea; promoted by high pH, high temperature, low CEC.

    Improved N Management Strategies

    • Deep placement of urea (reduces ammonia volatilization).
    • Neem-coated urea / urease inhibitors (NBPT) — slow hydrolysis.
    • Nitrification inhibitors (DCD, nitrapyrin) — reduce NO₃⁻ formation and leaching.
    • Split application synchronized with crop demand periods.
    • Fertigation with ammonium/urea solutions — precise timing and placement.
    • Use of cover crops and catch crops to prevent off-season NO₃⁻ leaching.
    • Site-specific N management using sensor-based (NDVI, SPAD) approaches.
  4. Explain Integrated Nutrient Management (INM). Discuss its principles and the role of biofertilizers in INM.
    Unit V | 10 marks
    Model Answer

    Integrated Nutrient Management (INM)

    INM is defined as the maintenance of soil fertility and plant nutrient supply at an optimum level for sustaining desired crop productivity through optimization of benefits from all possible sources of plant nutrients — chemical fertilizers, organic manures, crop residues, biofertilizers, and green manures — in an integrated manner.

    Principles of INM

    • Balance nutrient supply with crop demand to minimize surpluses and deficits.
    • Combine organic and inorganic nutrient sources to exploit synergistic effects.
    • Maintain or improve soil organic matter levels.
    • Minimize nutrient losses to environment (air, water).
    • Make use of indigenous nutrient resources (biological N fixation, mycorrhizae, crop residues).
    • Ensure economic viability and environmental sustainability.

    Components of INM

    • Inorganic fertilizers: To meet immediate nutrient demand; precise doses based on soil tests.
    • Organic manures: FYM, compost, vermicompost — improve soil physical/biological properties, supply micronutrients, slow-release nutrients.
    • Green manures: In-situ N fixation and organic matter addition (e.g., Sesbania adds 60–90 kg N/ha).
    • Crop residue management: Incorporation of straw to maintain organic matter and recycle nutrients.
    • Biofertilizers: Living microorganisms that enhance nutrient availability.

    Role of Biofertilizers

    • Rhizobium: Symbiotic N fixation in legumes; can fix 50–400 kg N/ha/yr depending on legume species.
    • Azotobacter and Azospirillum: Free-living and associative N fixers; contribute 15–30 kg N/ha and produce growth hormones.
    • Phosphate solubilizing bacteria (PSB): Bacillus megaterium, Pseudomonas striata — release bound P through organic acid production.
    • Mycorrhizal fungi (AM fungi): Increase P uptake surface dramatically; also assist in uptake of Zn, Cu, and water.
    • Blue-green algae (BGA) / Azolla: Important in rice cultivation; fix atmospheric N.

    Benefit of INM: Studies show that INM combining 50% RDF (recommended dose of fertilizer) + FYM or biofertilizer can achieve 80–100% of yields obtained with full chemical fertilizer alone, while improving soil health over time.

  5. Describe soil fertility evaluation methods including soil testing procedures. How are soil test results interpreted and used for fertilizer recommendation?
    Unit VI | 10 marks
    Model Answer

    Soil Fertility Evaluation

    Soil fertility evaluation involves assessing the soil's capacity to supply nutrients for crop production. Methods include:

    • Soil testing (chemical analysis)
    • Plant analysis (tissue testing)
    • Biological methods (pot experiments, bioassays)
    • Visual symptoms / indicator plants

    Soil Testing Procedure

    1. Soil sampling: Composite sample from 0–15 cm (surface) and 15–30 cm depth; sample from 10–15 spots in a zigzag/W pattern per field. 500 g mixed sample.
    2. Sample processing: Air-dry, crush, sieve through 2 mm sieve; grind portion through 0.5 mm for organic C and total N.
    3. Chemical extraction:
      • N: Alkaline KMnO₄ (Subbiah and Asija) — gives available N.
      • P: Olsen's method (NaHCO₃, neutral/alkaline soils); Bray-Kurtz (HCl + NH₄F, acid soils).
      • K: Neutral 1N ammonium acetate extraction; flame photometry.
      • Micronutrients: DTPA extraction (Lindsay and Norvell) for Fe, Mn, Zn, Cu.
      • pH: 1:2.5 soil:water suspension; EC in 1:2.5.
      • Organic C: Walkley–Black rapid titration method.

    Interpretation — Critical Levels

    Results are categorized as Low/Medium/High based on critical levels established by research. Example (for Indian soils):

    • Available N: Low <280, Medium 280–560, High >560 kg/ha
    • Available P₂O₅: Low <11, Medium 11–22, High >22 kg/ha
    • Available K₂O: Low <110, Medium 110–280, High >280 kg/ha
    • DTPA Zn: Deficient <0.6, Sufficient ≥0.6 mg/kg

    Fertilizer Recommendation Approaches

    • Targeted yield approach (STCR): Fertilizer dose = (Nutrient required for target yield − nutrient supplied by soil) / Fertilizer use efficiency. Developed by AICRP on STCR (ICAR).
    • General recommendation approach: Fixed doses for crops in agro-climatic zones based on research trials; does not account for individual field variability.
    • Nutrient balance approach: Replace nutrients removed by crop to maintain soil fertility status.

    Plant Analysis and Rapid Tests

    Plant tissue testing (most recently expanded leaf/petiole/grain) using spectrometric methods provides a mid-season check. Rapid spot tests use colorimetric reagents for field-level diagnosis of P, N, K, and micronutrient status. The SPAD chlorophyll meter gives a proxy for N status non-destructively. Indicator plants (certain weeds and volunteer species) with characteristic deficiency symptoms serve as bio-indicators of nutrient status.

Section II — Short-Long Questions [5 × 10 = 50 Marks]
  1. What is soil fertility? List any five factors affecting soil fertility.
    Unit I | Easy | 10 marks
    Answer

    Definition

    Soil fertility is the ability of soil to supply essential plant nutrients in adequate amounts, in available forms, and in proper balance for the growth of specified plants when other growth factors such as light, temperature, moisture, and physical conditions are favorable.

    Note: Soil productivity is a broader term that includes fertility plus other physical factors; a soil can be fertile but not productive if waterlogged or eroded.

    Five Key Factors Affecting Soil Fertility

    1. Soil pH: Controls nutrient availability, microbial activity, and chemical reactions. Most nutrients are available at pH 6–7.
    2. Organic matter content: Supplies nutrients through mineralization; improves CEC, water holding capacity, and microbial population.
    3. Soil texture and structure: Clayey soils have higher CEC and water retention; coarse soils are prone to leaching. Structure affects aeration and root growth.
    4. Microbial activity: Bacteria, fungi, and actinomycetes decompose organic matter and fix nitrogen; essential for nutrient cycling.
    5. Cropping history and management: Continuous cropping without replenishment depletes nutrients; crop rotation and manuring maintain fertility.

    Other factors: parent material, climate, drainage, topography, and fertilization history.

  2. Define deficiency symptoms. Describe the deficiency symptoms of nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, and iron in plants with examples.
    Unit II | Easy | 10 marks
    Answer

    Deficiency symptoms are abnormal changes in plant appearance (color, size, shape) caused by the inadequate supply of a specific essential element. They are used as a diagnostic tool in the field.

    Nitrogen Deficiency

    • Yellowing (chlorosis) of older/lower leaves first — N is mobile, moves from old to young leaves.
    • Stunted growth, thin stems, reduced tillering in cereals.
    • In maize: V-shaped yellowing starting from leaf tip downward.

    Phosphorus Deficiency

    • Purple or reddish coloration of older leaves (due to anthocyanin accumulation).
    • Delayed flowering and maturity; poor root development.
    • In maize: purple stems and undersides of leaves.

    Potassium Deficiency

    • Marginal leaf scorch (tip and margin necrosis) on older leaves first.
    • Weak stems, lodging; reduced fruit quality; premature leaf drop.
    • In potatoes: brown scorching of leaf margins.

    Iron Deficiency

    • Interveinal chlorosis of young leaves — veins remain green while tissue between turns yellow (Fe is immobile).
    • Severe deficiency: entire young leaf turns white/pale yellow.
    • Common in calcareous/alkaline soils ("lime-induced chlorosis").
    • Example: Iron deficiency in rice, groundnut, and sorghum on calcareous Vertisols.

    Key rule: Mobile nutrients (N, P, K, Mg) — symptoms appear on older leaves first. Immobile nutrients (Fe, Mn, Zn, Ca, B, Cu) — symptoms appear on younger/youngest leaves first.

  3. What are chemical fertilizers? Name and describe any three major phosphatic fertilizers, their P₂O₅ content, and their use in agriculture.
    Unit IV | Easy | 10 marks
    Answer

    Chemical Fertilizers

    Chemical (inorganic/synthetic) fertilizers are manufactured or mineral-derived materials that supply one or more essential plant nutrients in concentrated and readily available form. They are regulated under the Fertilizer (Control) Order, 1985.

    Three Major Phosphatic Fertilizers

    • 1. Single Superphosphate (SSP) — 16% P₂O₅
      Manufactured by treating rock phosphate with sulphuric acid. Also contains 12% S and 21% Ca — useful for S-deficient soils. Grey/white granule or powder. Water-soluble. Used as basal dose, especially in acid-to-neutral soils. Most economical source of P in India.
    • 2. Triple Superphosphate (TSP) — 44–46% P₂O₅
      Made by treating rock phosphate with phosphoric acid. Higher P concentration; no S. Used when high P is required with fewer cartage costs. Applied as basal dose; band placement preferred.
    • 3. Di-ammonium Phosphate (DAP) — 18% N + 46% P₂O₅
      Complex fertilizer; most widely used P fertilizer in India. Excellent granule, free-flowing; alkaline reaction on dissolution (transient NH₃ hazard near seeds — place 2–3 cm below seed). Provides both N and P; economical on a nutrient-cost basis.

    Note: For best P use efficiency, apply band placement, use at sowing as basal dose, and supplement with organic matter to reduce fixation.

  4. What is FYM (Farmyard Manure)? Describe its composition, preparation, and importance in agriculture.
    Unit V | Easy | 10 marks
    Answer

    Definition

    Farmyard manure (FYM) is the decomposed mixture of cattle dung, urine, litter (bedding material such as straw/hay), and leftover feed. It is the most traditional and widely used organic manure in Indian agriculture.

    Composition

    • Nitrogen: ~0.5%
    • Phosphorus (P₂O₅): ~0.25%
    • Potassium (K₂O): ~0.5%
    • Also supplies micronutrients (Zn, Fe, Mn, etc.) and humus-forming compounds.

    Nutrient content varies widely depending on animal, feed, handling, and decomposition stage.

    Preparation

    1. Cattle dung and urine collected along with litter (straw, leaves) in cattle sheds.
    2. Material is stored in a pit (preferably lined to prevent leaching) and covered to prevent volatilization of N and weathering losses.
    3. After 3–4 months of decomposition, well-rotted FYM (dark, crumbly, earthy) is ready for application.
    4. Fresh/raw FYM should not be applied directly — high C:N causes immobilization of soil N.

    Importance of FYM

    • Improves soil structure (aggregate stability, reduced compaction).
    • Increases water-holding capacity and aeration.
    • Supplies slow-release nutrients (balanced nutrition).
    • Enhances soil microbial activity and biodiversity.
    • Increases CEC, buffering capacity, and organic matter.
    • Improves response to chemical fertilizers — better NUE.
    • Recycles farm wastes; low-cost nutrient source.
  5. Name any five methods of fertilizer application. Briefly explain each method and state when each is most suitable.
    Unit VI | Easy | 10 marks
    Answer

    Methods of Fertilizer Application

    1. Broadcasting: Fertilizer is spread uniformly over the entire field surface before plowing (basal broadcast) or applied to standing crop (top-dress broadcast). Simple, suitable for closely spaced crops (wheat, paddy, pastures). Risk of volatilization and surface runoff.
    2. Band Placement (Localized): Fertilizer applied in bands/furrows near seed rows (2–5 cm beside and below seed — "side-dressed"). Reduces P fixation, improves root access. Ideal for P and K in row crops (maize, sugarcane, vegetables).
    3. Top-Dressing: Fertilizer applied to soil surface around growing crop without soil incorporation. Used for N fertilizers (urea, CAN) as split dose to standing crops. Risk of volatilization; irrigate immediately after application.
    4. Fertigation: Fertilizer dissolved in irrigation water and applied through drip or sprinkler systems. Highly efficient; precise timing and placement; ideal for high-value crops (vegetables, orchards). Requires soluble fertilizers (urea, KNO₃, MAP).
    5. Foliar Application: Dilute fertilizer solution sprayed on leaves. Used for micronutrient correction (Zn, Fe, Mn, B) and quick N supplementation (2% urea spray). Bypasses soil fixation reactions. Effective but costly; not suitable for large doses.

    Bonus: Other methods — seed soaking, root dipping (for transplanted crops), soil injection (liquid NH₃), deep placement (paddy/upland rice for urea briquettes).
